IMPORTANT QUESTIONS

Short Queston Answers

  • Why does the resistance of a p-n junction diode decrease when it is forward biased?
    When a p-n junction diode is forward biased, the applied voltage reduces the barrier potential, allowing more charge carriers to cross the junction, thus decreasing the resistance.
  • Define knee voltage. What is the value of knee voltage for Si and Ge diodes?
    Knee voltage is the minimum forward voltage at which a diode starts to conduct significantly. For silicon (Si) diodes, it is approximately 0.7V, and for germanium (Ge) diodes, it is approximately 0.3V
  • Why is the base region of a transistor very thin compared to the emitter and collector regions?
    The base region is thin to ensure that most charge carriers injected from the emitter reach the collector, enhancing the transistor's current amplification
  • What are photodiodes?
    Photodiodes are semiconductor devices that convert light into electrical current. They operate in reverse bias and generate a current proportional to the incident light intensity.
  • Differentiate between ordinary and light-emitting diodes (LEDs).
    Ordinary diodes allow current to flow in one direction and block it in the opposite direction. LEDs emit light when forward biased, converting electrical energy into light energy
  • Why does Zener breakdown voltage decrease with an increase in temperature?
    Zener breakdown voltage decreases with an increase in temperature because the increase in temperature reduces the energy gap of the semiconductor material, making it easier for electrons to break free.
  • Define ripple factor of a rectifier.
    Ripple factor is the measure of the residual AC component in the output of a rectifier. It is defined as the ratio of the RMS value of the AC component to the DC component in the rectified output.
  • Why do we prefer to use transistor amplifiers in common-emitter (CE) mode?
    Transistor amplifiers in CE mode provide high voltage gain, current gain, and power gain, making them suitable for amplification purposes
  • Define peak inverse voltage and leakage current.
    • • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): The maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand without breaking down.
    • • Leakage Current: The small current that flows through a diode when it is reverse biased

  • What is a time base circuit?
    A time base circuit generates a linearly increasing or decreasing voltage over time, often used in oscilloscopes to provide a stable time reference for the horizontal axis
  • What is a voltage multiplier circuit?
    A voltage multiplier circuit converts AC voltage to a higher DC voltage using diodes and capacitors, commonly used in applications requiring high voltage from a low voltage source
  • What are h-parameters in a circuit?
    h-parameters (hybrid parameters) are a set of four parameters used to describe the behavior of a transistor in terms of input impedance, output impedance, forward current gain, and reverse voltage gain.
  • Why does a half-wave rectifier have lower efficiency than a full-wave rectifier?
    A half-wave rectifier only uses one half of the AC cycle, resulting in lower efficiency and higher ripple compared to a full-wave rectifier, which uses both halves of the AC cycle.
  • Define thermal runaway.
    Thermal runaway is a condition where an increase in temperature causes a further increase in temperature, leading to the destruction of a semiconductor device due to excessive current flow.
  • Explain the need for doping in a transistor.
    Doping introduces impurities into a semiconductor to create regions with different charge carrier concentrations, essential for forming the p-n junctions in a transistor and controlling its electrical properties.
  • Explain why a good rectifier should have a low ripple factor.
    A low ripple factor indicates that the rectifier produces a smooth DC output with minimal AC components, which is desirable for stable and efficient power supply performance
  • Define reverse saturation current.
    Reverse saturation current is the small current that flows through a diode when it is reverse biased, primarily due to minority carriers.
  • Define peak inverse voltage (PIV).
    Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) is the maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand without breaking down.
  • Explain why a transistor is a current-controlled device.
    A transistor is a current-controlled device because the output current (collector current) is controlled by the input current (base current), allowing it to amplify signals.

  • What is an ideal voltage source?
    An ideal voltage source is a theoretical device that provides a constant voltage regardless of the current drawn from it, with zero internal resistance.
  • State Thevenin's theorem.
    hevenin's theorem states that any linear electrical network with voltage and current sources and resistances can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a single voltage source in series with a resistance.
  • Name two applications of CRO.
    • • Measuring and displaying electrical waveforms.
    • • Analyzing the frequency and amplitude of signals.
  • What is a positive clipper?
    A positive clipper is a circuit that removes or "clips" the positive portion of an input signal, allowing only the negative portion to pass through.
  • Why is the space charge region called the depletion region?
    The space charge region is called the depletion region because it is depleted of free charge carriers (electrons and holes) due to the formation of a p-n junction.
  • Define peak inverse voltage
    Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) is the maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand without breaking down.
  • How does the operating point of a transistor amplifier change with temperature?
    The operating point of a transistor amplifier shifts with temperature due to changes in the transistor's current gain and leakage currents, potentially leading to thermal runaway if not properly managed.
  • hat are the disadvantages of transistors?
    • • Susceptibility to thermal runaway.
    • • Limited power handling capacity compared to vacuum tubes.
    • • Sensitivity to radiation and electromagnetic interference.
  • What is a solar cell?
    A solar cell is a semiconductor device that converts light energy into electrical energy through the photovoltaic effect.
  • What is the principle of LED?
    The principle of an LED (Light Emitting Diode) is electroluminescence, where the recombination of electrons and holes in a semiconductor material releases energy in the form of light.

  • How is a voltage source converted into a current source?
    A voltage source can be converted into a current source by connecting it in series with a high-value resistor. The current source will then provide a constant current equal to the voltage of the source divided by the resistance.
  • How does a Zener diode differ from an ordinary diode?
    A Zener diode is designed to operate in reverse breakdown mode, maintaining a constant voltage across it, while an ordinary diode is designed to conduct current in the forward direction and block it in the reverse direction.
  • What is the need for doping a pure semiconductor?
    Doping a pure semiconductor introduces impurities to increase the number of free charge carriers, enhancing its electrical conductivity and enabling the formation of p-n junctions for various electronic devices.
  • Define diffusion capacitance of a p-n junction.
    Diffusion capacitance is the capacitance due to the storage of charge carriers in the depletion region of a p-n junction, which varies with the applied voltage.
  • Can we interchange the emitter and collector of a transistor?
    No, the emitter and collector of a transistor cannot be interchanged because they are doped differently and have different physical structures, affecting their performance and functionality.
  • Explain why a good rectifier should have a low ripple factor.
    A low ripple factor indicates that the rectifier produces a smooth DC output with minimal AC components, which is desirable for stable and efficient power supply performance.
  • What are clipping circuits?
    Clipping circuits are electronic circuits that remove or "clip" portions of an input signal that exceed a certain threshold, shaping the output waveform.
  • What do you mean by thermal runaway of a transistor?
    Thermal runaway is a condition where an increase in temperature causes a further increase in temperature, leading to the destruction of a transistor due to excessive current flow.
  • What is PIV of a full-wave rectifier?
    Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) of a full-wave rectifier is the maximum reverse voltage that each diode must withstand, which is equal to twice the peak input voltage.
  • How is the conductivity of a semiconductor affected by a rise in temperature?
    The conductivity of a semiconductor increases with a rise in temperature because the thermal energy generates more charge carriers (electrons and holes), enhancing its electrical conductivity

  • What is the charge on an n-type semiconductor?
    An n-type semiconductor has an excess of electrons, making the majority charge carriers negative (electrons).
  • What is Zener breakdown? What is its cause?
    Zener breakdown occurs in a Zener diode when the reverse voltage exceeds a certain value, causing a sharp increase in current. It is caused by the strong electric field breaking the covalent bonds, generating electron-hole pairs.
  • What is a negative clipper?
    A negative clipper is a circuit that removes or "clips" the negative portion of an input signal, allowing only the positive portion to pass through.
  • What do you understand by load line?
    A load line is a graphical representation of all possible combinations of current and voltage in a circuit, showing the relationship between the output current and voltage for a given load.
  • What is the advantage of a bridge rectifier over a conventional full-wave rectifier?
    A bridge rectifier does not require a center-tapped transformer and provides better transformer utilization, resulting in higher efficiency and reduced cost.
  • Describe the action of the inductor filter and its limitations.
    An inductor filter smooths the output of a rectifier by allowing DC to pass while blocking AC components. Its limitations include large size, weight, and reduced effectiveness at low frequencies.
  • Why is a BJT called a current-controlled device?
    Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) is called a current-controlled device because the output current (collector current) is controlled by the input current (base current).
  • Why is a capacitor filter preferred over an inductor filter?
    A capacitor filter is preferred because it is smaller, lighter, and more effective at filtering high-frequency components compared to an inductor filter.
  • What is the leakage current in a transistor?
    Leakage current is the small current that flows through a transistor when it is in the off state, primarily due to minority carriers.

    The Early effect is the variation in the width of the base region of a transistor due to changes in the collector-base voltage, leading to an increase in the collector current and a decrease in the current gain.
